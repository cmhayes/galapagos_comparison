Galapagos Comparison
Located in the Pacific Ocean off the coast of Ecuador, the Galapagos Islands is known for its rich biodiversity that inspired Charles Darwin's theory of evolution. The volcanic archipelago consists of 18 main islands and is one of the few places on Earth that has remained largely untouched by human inhabitants, as 97% is national park.

  • Weather
    While the Galapagos is located on the Equator, the Humboldt Current carries cooler water to the islands, translating to a moist, often rainy, climate. In addition to frequent rain and high humidity, the Galapagos is often affected by El Nino events, which occur every 3-7 years and are associated with warm water temperatures, a depletion of nutrients in the water and a rise in the sea level. For most years, the cooler season, which is referred to as garua, runs from June to November, while the warm season stretches from December to May. Because the islands vary in altitude, location and terrain, the weather varies as well.

    Similarly, because the United States has such varied landscapes and regions, the climate and weather varies substantially. While certain states frequently receive snow and rain, others are characterized by extensive dry seasons and excessive heat.

  • Biodiversity
    Lauded for being a "natural laboratory", the Galapagos Islands is home to some of the highest levels of endemism in the world. 80% of land birds, 97% of reptiles and land mammals, 20% of marine species, and more than 30% of plants found on the islands are endemic and are considered some of the most valuable assets of the islands. However, due to exponential growth of residents, vehicles and ships, the Ecuadorian Government issued Presidential Decree N 270 in April 2007, declaring that the Galapagos Archipelago is at risk and its conservation is a national priority. The Galapagos Report of 2009-2010 reiterated the government's sentiment, concluding:

    "Galapagos is not a paradise separate from the rest of the world. It is a constellation of very fragile ecosystems that now requires special care and attention to ensure that the forces of globalization do not overcome its capacity to assimilate, adapt, and recuperate from natural and human-made disturbances." (Tapia, et. al, p.12)

    The United States, on the other hand, only has 27.08% of its land marked as nationally protected area. Despite the disparity in percentages, the United States also faces conservation issues. In fact, the Nature Conservancy concluded that in one of every four states, more than 10% of native species are at risk.

  • Economics
    As it becomes an increasingly popular vacation destination, the Galapagos relies heavily on tourism, which comprises 53% of its revenue. Many of the most successful residents are those who own souvenir shops close to the shore near cruise ship docks and the Charles Darwin Research Station. In a survey conducted by the Charles Darwin Foundation in 2009 of 100 business owners in Puerto Ayora - the largest city in the Galapagos - the general consensus was made that employers would rather hire employees from the mainland rather than individuals native to the Galapagos because "local workers charge more and don't work well." (Villarreal & Grenier, p. 115). In general, researchers and academics alike have concluded that the Galapagos economy still has much room for improvement and growth. As one researcher articulates:

    "There appears to be no integration of the industry, its employees, and its economic trajectory into a long - term vision that values sustainable and island-appropriate development." (Villarreal & Grenier, pg. 120)

    While the Galapagos relies almost entirely on one industry to generate revenues and jobs for its residents, the United States offers more variety, generating significant revenues from numerous industries, including finance, insurance, real estate, retail trade, educational services and manufacturing. The United States also generates significantly larger revenues than the Galapagos and boasts a higher median household income.

  • Education
    The United States (99%) and the Galapagos (98.7%) boast similarly high levels of literary and low student-teacher ratios. However, the numbers do not accurately paint the full picture of the education system in the Galapagos. Currently, there are only 20 educational institutions in the Galapagos serving roughly 5,200 primary and secondary students. Many problems have plagued the education system on the islands, including a weak teacher base, weak school infrastructure, and the absence of a voice in civil society addressing the educational needs and coordinating progressive initiatives. In this regard, the Galapagos is far behind the United States in education and could gain from reforming its current system.

  • Health
    Some of the largest discrepancies in the quality of life in the Galapagos and in the United States stem from the disparities in the quality of health care. Ecuador offers free public health care to its citizens, which explains why the Galapagos allocates a much larger percentage of its budget to health care needs. However, it is important to note that while the states set aside a smaller portion of their budgets to health care, they spend significantly more on health expenditures. Accordingly, the United States boasts numerous doctors, advanced equipment, and clean facilities, while the Galapagos lacks all three elements. In a 2009-2010 report on the economic and social state of the Galapagos, researchers discovered:

    "Most of those surveyed agreed that they do not trust doctors working in Galapagos, and that 'any illness more serious than a cold should be treated on the mainland.' All agreed that health care in Galapagos is poor and more should be done to attract doctors and specialists to the islands." (Villarreal & Grenier, p. 118)
